被 — Passive Voice

被 (bèi) marks the passive voice in Chinese. The basic pattern is: subject + 被 + agent + verb + complement. Unlike English, the passive in Chinese often carries a negative or undesirable connotation — something bad or unwanted happened to the subject. Because of this, Chinese speakers frequently prefer the active voice or constructions with 让 (ràng) or 叫 (jiào) for neutral events.

Structure: Subject + 被 + Agent + Verb + Complement

ChinesePinyinEnglish
他被老板批评了。Tā bèi lǎobǎn pīpíng le.He was criticized by his boss.
我的钱包被偷了。Wǒ de qiánbāo bèi tōu le.My wallet was stolen.
这个项目被取消了。Zhège xiàngmù bèi qǔxiāo le.The project was cancelled.
她被朋友误解了。Tā bèi péngyǒu wùjiě le.She was misunderstood by her friend.

The Complement Requirement

In a 被 sentence, the verb almost always needs a complement or result word after it — a bare verb sounds incomplete. The complement can be 了 (le), a resultative verb, or a direction complement.

  • 报告被他改好了。 Bàogào bèi tā gǎi hǎo le. — The report was corrected by him.
  • 窗户被风吹开了。 Chuānghù bèi fēng chuī kāi le. — The window was blown open by the wind.

Passive vs. Active Voice

Chinese strongly prefers the active voice for neutral or positive events. Use 被 mainly when the subject suffers or something goes wrong. For everyday statements, rephrase as active.

Passive (被)Active (preferred)
蛋糕被他吃掉了。他把蛋糕吃掉了。
Tā bèi tā chī diào le.Tā bǎ dàngāo chī diào le.
The cake was eaten by him.He ate the cake (up).

让 and 叫 as Softer Passives

让 (ràng) and 叫 (jiào) can also express a passive meaning in colloquial speech. They are more neutral in tone than 被.

  • 我的自行车让人骑走了。 Wǒ de zìxíngchē ràng rén qí zǒu le. — My bicycle was ridden off by someone.
  • 他叫警察带走了。 Tā jiào jǐngchá dài zǒu le. — He was taken away by the police.
