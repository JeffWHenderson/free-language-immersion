Broken Plurals — Intro

Arabic plurals are one of the most fascinating — and challenging — aspects of the language. Unlike English (add -s) or Spanish (add -s/-es), Arabic often forms plurals by changing the internal vowel pattern of the word. These are called broken plurals (جمع التكسير, jam' at-taksir) because the word's structure is "broken" and reformed.

There is no single rule that predicts all broken plural patterns — they must be memorized. However, several very common patterns cover a large number of everyday words.

Sound Plurals (Regular Plurals)

Not all Arabic plurals are broken. Sound plurals (جمع سالم, jam' salim) add a suffix and are more predictable:

  • مهندس → مهندسون / مهندسين engineer → engineers (m.) (muhandis → muhandisun / muhandisin)
  • مدرّسة → مدرّسات teacher (f.) → teachers (f.) (mudarrisa → mudarrisat)
  • طالبة → طالبات student (f.) → students (f.) (taliba → talibat)

The Non-Human Plural Rule

A crucial Arabic grammar rule: plural nouns that refer to non-humans (objects, animals) take feminine singular agreement in verbs and adjectives:

  • الكتب جديدة. The books are new. (al-kutub jadida) — not جدد
  • الأسنان مؤلمة. The teeth are painful. (al-asnan mu'lima)
  • مفاصلي متصلبة. My joints are stiff. (mafasili mutasallaba)

The Dual

Arabic also has a special dual form for exactly two of something, formed by adding ان (an) to the singular:

  • يد → يدان hand → two hands (yad → yadan)
  • عين → عينان eye → two eyes ('ayn → 'aynan)
  • بيضة → بيضتان egg → two eggs (bayda → baydatan)

Common Pitfalls

  • Learn plurals with singulars: When learning a new Arabic noun, always learn its plural at the same time. Dictionaries always list the plural form.
  • No rule covers everything: There are about 30 broken plural patterns in Arabic. The most productive strategy is to learn the most common patterns and memorize the rest as vocabulary items.
  • Non-human agreement: When talking about a group of objects, always use feminine singular agreement: الأسنان بيضاء (the teeth are white, NOT أبيض).
  • Dual vs plural: The dual is only for exactly two. For three or more, use the regular plural.
