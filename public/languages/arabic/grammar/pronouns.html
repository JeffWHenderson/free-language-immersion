الضمائر — Personal Pronouns

Arabic has a rich pronoun system. Every pronoun is specific about gender (masculine or feminine) and number (singular, dual, or plural). As a beginner, focus on the singular forms first — they cover the vast majority of everyday speech.

A key feature of Arabic: pronouns are often dropped when the verb makes the subject clear. But they are used for emphasis or clarity, and in verbless (equational) sentences they are essential.

Examples

  • أنا طالب. I am a student. (ana taalib)
  • أنا طالبة. I am a student. (ana taaliba) — feminine speaker
  • هو من مصر. He is from Egypt. (huwa min misr)
  • هي مدرّسة. She is a teacher. (hiya mudarrisa)
  • نحن أصدقاء. We are friends. (nahnu asdiqa')
  • هم يأكلون معاً. They eat together. (hum ya'kulun ma'an)
  • أنتَ تتكلم العربية جيداً. You speak Arabic well. (anta tatakallam al-arabiyya jayyidan) — to a man
  • أنتِ تتكلمين العربية جيداً. You speak Arabic well. (anti tatakallamin al-arabiyya jayyidan) — to a woman

Common Pitfalls

  • أنتَ vs أنتِ: These look nearly identical in unvowelled text. Context and the listener's gender clarify which to use. When speaking, the vowel difference (a vs i) is important.
  • No gender-neutral pronoun: Every Arabic noun is masculine or feminine. "It" is translated as هو or هي depending on the noun's grammatical gender — not its natural gender.
  • The dual: Arabic uses a special form for exactly two people: هما (huma) = the two of them, أنتما (antuma) = you two. This is unique among major world languages and worth learning early.
  • Pronoun dropping: In verbal sentences, the verb conjugation already encodes the subject, so pronouns are often omitted: أذهب = I go (not أنا أذهب, though that's also correct and adds emphasis).
