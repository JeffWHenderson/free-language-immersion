المذكر والمؤنث — Masculine & Feminine

Every Arabic noun is either masculine (مذكر, mudhakkar) or feminine (مؤنث, mu'annath). This grammatical gender affects adjectives, verbs, pronouns, and demonstratives — so getting a feel for it early is essential.

How to Identify Feminine Nouns

The most reliable marker of a feminine noun is the ending ة (ta marbuta, pronounced "-a" or "-at"). Most nouns ending in ة are feminine.

Gender Agreement in Sentences

Adjectives, demonstratives, and verbs all agree with the noun's gender:

  • الخبز طازج. The bread is fresh. (masc.) (al-khubz tazij)
  • القهوة طازجة. The coffee is fresh. (fem.) (al-qahwa tazija)
  • هذا الكتاب جيد. This book is good. (masc.) (hatha al-kitab jayyid)
  • هذه الطاولة كبيرة. This table is big. (fem.) (hathihi at-tawila kabira)
  • الدجاج لذيذ. The chicken is delicious. (masc.) (ad-dajaj ladhidh)
  • الوجبة لذيذة. The meal is delicious. (fem.) (al-wajba ladhidha)
  • أنا سعيد. / أنا سعيدة. I am happy. (m.) / I am happy. (f.) (ana sa'id / ana sa'ida)

Common Pitfalls

  • The ة rule is not absolute: Not all feminine nouns end in ة, and a few masculine words technically end in sounds resembling it. Always check when in doubt.
  • Adjective always follows the noun: Arabic adjectives come after the noun they describe, and they must match it in gender, number, and definiteness.
  • Professions: When a woman holds a profession, add ة to the masculine form: طبيب → طبيبة. When saying "I am a doctor," the adjective/noun still reflects your own gender: أنا طبيب (m.) / أنا طبيبة (f.).
