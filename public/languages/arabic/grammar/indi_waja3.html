عندي وجع — Expressing Pain

Knowing how to describe pain and discomfort is essential for any traveler or language learner. Arabic has several natural patterns for this, combining the possessive construction عندي ('indi = I have) with the word for pain, or using the verb يؤلم (yu'lim = to hurt/pain).

Pattern 1: عندي وجع في + Body Part

The most colloquial and widely understood pattern uses عندي (I have) + وجع (waja' = pain/ache) + في (fi = in) + the body part:

ArabicTransliterationMeaning
عندي وجع في رأسي.'indi waja' fi ra'siI have a headache.
عندي وجع في بطني.'indi waja' fi batniI have a stomachache.
عندي وجع في ظهري.'indi waja' fi dahriI have a backache.
عندي وجع في أسناني.'indi waja' fi asnaniI have a toothache.
عندي وجع في صدري.'indi waja' fi sadriI have chest pain.

Pattern 2: Body Part + يؤلمني / تؤلمني

This pattern means "[body part] is hurting me." يؤلمني (yu'limuni) is for masculine nouns; تؤلمني (tu'limuni) is for feminine nouns:

ArabicTransliterationMeaningGender
رأسي يؤلمني.ra'si yu'limuniMy head hurts.masc. (رأس)
ظهري يؤلمني.dahri yu'limuniMy back hurts.masc. (ظهر)
أذني تؤلمني.udhni tu'limuniMy ear hurts.fem. (أذن)
ساقي تؤلمني.saqi tu'limuniMy leg hurts.fem. (ساق)
بطني يؤلمني.batni yu'limuniMy stomach hurts.masc. (بطن)

Pattern 3: عندي ألم في + Body Part

More formal, using ألم (alam = pain) instead of وجع (waja' = ache/pain — more colloquial):

  • عندي ألم في الصدر. I have chest pain. ('indi alam fi as-sadr)
  • عندي ألم في الرقبة. I have neck pain. ('indi alam fi ar-raqaba)

Useful Phrases at the Doctor or Pharmacy

  • أشعر بألم. I feel pain. (ash'ur bi-alam)
  • أشعر بتوعك. I feel unwell / I feel sick. (ash'ur bi-tawa''uk)
  • عندي حرارة. I have a fever. ('indi harara)
  • عندي سعال. I have a cough. ('indi su'al)
  • أين يؤلمك؟ Where does it hurt (you)? (ayna yu'limuk?) — doctor to patient
  • منذ متى وأنت تؤلمك؟ How long have you been in pain? (mundhu mata wa-anta tu'limuk?)
  • أحتاج إلى طبيب. I need a doctor. (ahtaj ila tabib)
  • هل عندك دواء للصداع؟ Do you have medicine for a headache? (hal 'indak dawa' lis-suda'?)

عندي vs لديّ

Both عندي ('indi) and لديّ (ladayya) mean "I have." عندي is more colloquial and more common in Levantine Arabic; لديّ is slightly more formal. Either is correct for expressing pain.

Common Pitfalls

  • وجع vs ألم: وجع (waja') is the colloquial word for pain/ache, widely used in Levantine and Egyptian Arabic. ألم (alam) is the formal/MSA term. Both are understood everywhere.
  • Verb gender agreement: يؤلمني is used for masculine body part nouns; تؤلمني for feminine ones. Check the gender of the body part: رأس (head) is masculine, ساق (leg) is feminine.
  • Possessive suffix on body part: The ي- suffix on the body part means "my": رأسي = my head, ظهري = my back, أذني = my ear. This suffix causes the word to change slightly in pronunciation.
