الَّذِي / الَّتِي — Relative Pronouns

Arabic relative pronouns — equivalent to English "who," "which," and "that" — agree with their antecedent in gender and number. The main relative pronouns are الَّذِي (alladhī, m.s.), الَّتِي (allatī, f.s.), الَّذِينَ (alladhīna, m.pl.), and اللَّوَاتِي (allawātī, f.pl.).

Definite Antecedent Required

A relative pronoun is only used when the antecedent is definite (has الـ or a possessive suffix). With an indefinite antecedent, the relative clause is attached directly using a verb or active participle — no relative pronoun word:

  • الطَّبِيبُ الَّذِي يُعَالِجُنِي al-ṭabību lladhī yuʿālijnī — the doctor who treats me (definite: الطَّبِيبُ)
  • طَبِيبٌ يُعَالِجُنِي ṭabībun yuʿālijnī — a doctor who treats me (indefinite: no relative pronoun)

Resumptive Pronouns

When the relative pronoun functions as the object of a verb or preposition in the relative clause, a resumptive pronoun is added to point back to the antecedent:

  • الْعَمَلُ الَّذِي أَقُومُ بِهِ al-ʿamalu lladhī aqūmu bihi — the work that I do (bihi = 'by/with it', referring back to العمل)
  • الشَّرِكَةُ الَّتِي أَعْمَلُ فِيهَا al-sharikatu llatī aʿmalu fīhā — the company where I work (fīhā = 'in it', feminine)
  • الزَّمِيلُ الَّذِي سَاعَدَنِي al-zamīlu lladhī sāʿadanī — the colleague who helped me (nī = me, direct object suffix)

Inanimate Plurals

In Arabic grammar, inanimate (non-human) plurals are treated as feminine singular. Therefore الَّتِي is used for inanimate plural antecedents:

  • الْكُتُبُ الَّتِي قَرَأْتُهَا al-kutubu llatī qaraʾtuhā — the books that I read (الكتب is plural but inanimate → الَّتِي)

Example Sentences

  • الطَّبِيبُ الَّذِي يُعَالِجُ الْمَرْضَى. al-ṭabību lladhī yuʿāliju l-marḍā — the doctor who treats patients.
  • الْوَظِيفَةُ الَّتِي أُحِبُّهَا. al-waẓīfatu llatī uḥibbuhā — the job that I love.
  • الزُّمَلَاءُ الَّذِينَ عَمِلُوا مَعِي. al-zumalāʾu lladhīna ʿamilū maʿī — the colleagues who worked with me.
  • النِّسَاءُ اللَّوَاتِي تَقَدَّمْنَ لِلْوَظِيفَة. al-nisāʾu llawātī taqaddamna lil-waẓīfa — the women who applied for the job.
