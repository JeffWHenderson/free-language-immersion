Time Words & Days — ظروف الزمن والأيام

Arabic has a rich set of time words that work as adverbs. Most appear without any special marker — just drop them into the sentence at the beginning or end. Unlike English, position is flexible: a time word can come first for emphasis or last as a simple addition.

Flexible Word Order

Time words can appear at the start of a sentence (for emphasis) or at the end (as simple context). Both are correct and natural.

Time word first (emphasis)
اليوم أنا مشغول. al-yawm ana mashghūl. — Today I am busy.
Time word last (neutral)
أنا مشغول اليوم. ana mashghūl al-yawm. — I am busy today.

Days of the Week

The Arabic week begins on Sunday (الأحد), not Monday. Friday (يوم الجمعة) is the day of the communal Friday prayer (صلاة الجمعة, ṣalāt al-jumʿa) and is the main day off across most Arabic-speaking countries. Saturday–Sunday is the standard weekend in many Arab states today, though Friday–Saturday is common in the Gulf.

ArabicRomanizedEnglish
الأحدal-aḥadSunday
الاثنينal-ithnainMonday
الثلاثاءal-thulāthāʾTuesday
الأربعاءal-arbaʿāʾWednesday
الخميسal-khamīsThursday
الجمعةal-jumʿaFriday
السبتal-sabtSaturday

To say "on Monday," use يوم الاثنين (yawm al-ithnain = day of Monday), or simply الاثنين.

Frequency Words

ArabicRomanizedEnglish
دائماًdāʾimanalways
أحياناًaḥyānansometimes
نادراًnādiranrarely
أبداًabadannever (used with negation)

أبداً (abadan = never) is always paired with a negative verb: لا أذهب أبداً (lā adhhab abadan = I never go). Alone, أبداً in a response means "absolutely not."

Example Sentences

  • غداً يوم الجمعة. ghadan yawm al-jumʿa. — Tomorrow is Friday.
  • أشرب القهوة دائماً في الصباح. ashrab al-qahwa dāʾiman fi al-ṣabāḥ. — I always drink coffee in the morning.
  • هل أنت حر يوم الأحد؟ hal anta ḥurr yawm al-aḥad? — Are you free on Sunday?
  • سأذهب إلى السوق بعد الظهر. sa-adhhab ilā al-sūq baʿd al-ẓuhr. — I will go to the market this afternoon.
