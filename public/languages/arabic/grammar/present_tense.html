المضارع — Present Tense Verbs

Arabic verbs are built on a system of roots — typically three consonants — combined with prefixes and suffixes to show tense, person, gender, and number. The present tense (المضارع, al-mudari') describes ongoing or habitual actions and is formed with a consistent pattern of prefixes (and sometimes suffixes) added to the verb stem.

Present Tense Conjugation Pattern

Let's look at the verb كتب (k-t-b = to write), with the present stem كتُب → يكتب (yaktub):

The prefixes to memorize: أ- (ana/I), تـ- (you/he/she), يـ- (he/they-m.), نـ- (we)

Examples in Context

  • أعمل في مستشفى. I work in a hospital. (a'mal fi mustashfa)
  • هو يدرس الطب. He studies medicine. (huwa yadrus at-tibb)
  • هي تتكلم العربية بطلاقة. She speaks Arabic fluently. (hiya tatakallam al-arabiyya bi-talaqah)
  • نذهب إلى المكتب كل يوم. We go to the office every day. (nadhhab ila al-maktab kull yawm)
  • ماذا تفعل؟ What are you doing? (matha taf'al?)
  • أتعلّم العربية. I am learning Arabic. (ata'allam al-arabiyya)
  • هم يلعبون في الحديقة. They play in the park. (hum yal'abun fi al-hadiqa)

The Root System

Arabic verbs share roots across related words. Once you know the root, you can recognize many words:

  • كتب (k-t-b): كتب (kataba = he wrote), يكتب (yaktub = he writes), كتاب (kitab = book), كاتب (katib = writer), مكتب (maktab = office/desk), مكتبة (maktaba = library)
  • درس (d-r-s): درس (darasa = he studied), يدرس (yadrus = he studies), درس (dars = lesson), مدرسة (madrasa = school), مدرّس (mudarris = teacher)

Common Pitfalls

  • No progressive form: Arabic present tense covers both "I write" and "I am writing." Context clarifies which is meant.
  • She/you(m.) look the same: Both هي and أنتَ take the prefix تـ. The forms for "she writes" and "you (m.) write" are both تكتب. Pronouns or context distinguish them.
  • Verb first: In formal/written Arabic, verbs often come before the subject: يذهب أحمد (Ahmad goes). In spoken Levantine Arabic, subject-verb order is also very common: أحمد بيروح.
