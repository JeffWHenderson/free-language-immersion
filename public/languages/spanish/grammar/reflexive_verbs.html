Reflexive Verbs — se, me, te

A reflexive verb shows that the subject does the action to themselves. In Spanish, reflexive verbs are marked with reflexive pronouns (me, te, se, nos, se) that match the subject. The infinitive form ends in -se: lavarse, ducharse, sentirse.

Placement of reflexive pronouns

The reflexive pronoun goes before the conjugated verb. With an infinitive, it can attach to the end.

  • Me lavo la cara. / Voy a lavarme la cara. I wash my face. / I'm going to wash my face.
  • Te cepillas los dientes. / Debes cepillarte los dientes. You brush your teeth. / You should brush your teeth.

Doler — a special case (indirect object, not reflexive)

Doler (to hurt/ache) works like gustar: the body part is the grammatical subject, and an indirect object pronoun shows who is hurting. It is not truly reflexive but is closely related to body-care language.

  • Me duele la cabeza. My head hurts. (literally: the head hurts me)
  • Le duelen las rodillas. His/her knees hurt.
  • ¿Te duele el estómago? Does your stomach hurt?

Body parts use the definite article, not possessives

With reflexive verbs and body parts, Spanish uses la, el, los, las — not mi, tu — because the reflexive pronoun already shows possession.

  • Lávate las manos. Wash your hands. (not "tus manos")
  • Me rompí el brazo. I broke my arm.
