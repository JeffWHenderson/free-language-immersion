Word Patterns — Spotting How Spanish Works

Spanish has a set of predictable patterns — suffixes, prefixes, and endings — that repeat constantly. Once you recognize them, you can decode thousands of unfamiliar words and build new ones on the fly.

-ado / -ido — Past Participles & Adjectives

Drop the infinitive ending and add -ado (-ar verbs) or -ido (-er/-ir verbs) to form the past participle. Used with haber in perfect tenses, and also as adjectives with estar.

  • He comido mucho hoy. I have eaten a lot today. (perfect tense)
  • La puerta está abierta. The door is open. (adjective use)
  • El trabajo está hecho. The work is done. (adjective use)
  • El vaso está roto. The glass is broken.
Irregular past participles must be memorized — but there are only about a dozen common ones. The good news: they double as everyday adjectives, so you'll see them constantly.

-mente — Turning Adjectives into Adverbs

Add -mente to the feminine singular form of an adjective to create an adverb. This is the Spanish equivalent of English -ly.

  • Habla muy rápidamente. She speaks very quickly.
  • Todo salió perfectamente. Everything turned out perfectly.
  • Fácilmente el mejor plato. Easily the best dish.
For adjectives that already end in -e or a consonant (like fácil, feliz), just add -mente directly — there is no separate feminine form to worry about.

-ción / -sión — Abstract Nouns

Many Spanish verbs become nouns ending in -ción or -sión. These are almost always feminine and map directly onto English -tion / -sion words.

  • Fue una gran celebración. It was a great celebration.
  • ¿Puedes explicar la situación? Can you explain the situation?
If you see a word ending in -ción or -sión, it is always feminine: la comunicación, la televisión. When in doubt, try swapping the Spanish ending for English -tion — you'll be right most of the time.

-ísimo/a — Extreme Intensifier

Add -ísimo (masculine) or -ísima (feminine) to the stem of an adjective to express an extreme degree — the equivalent of "extremely" or "very very." The adjective agrees in gender and number.

Adjective-ísimo formEnglish
buenobuenísimoabsolutely great
ricoriquísimoincredibly delicious / rich
fácilfacilísimoextremely easy
carocarísimoextremely expensive
grandegrandísimoenormous
cansadocansadísimoabsolutely exhausted
  • Este restaurante es buenísimo. This restaurant is absolutely fantastic.
  • Estoy cansadísima. I am completely exhausted. (feminine speaker)
  • ¡La paella estaba riquísima! The paella was incredibly delicious!
Note the spelling change in rico → riquísimo (c → qu before i to preserve the hard-k sound). Similar changes apply to other adjectives ending in -co, -go, or -z.

Prefix Patterns: des-, re-, in-/im-

Spanish shares several productive prefixes with English. Spotting them lets you guess the meaning of words you've never seen before.

PrefixMeaningExamplesEnglish
des-reversal / negationdeshacerto undo

in- / im-not (negation)imposibleimpossible

  • Tuve que deshacer todo el trabajo. I had to undo all the work.
  • El wi-fi se desconectó otra vez. The wi-fi disconnected again.
  • Es imposible llegar a tiempo. It's impossible to arrive on time.
  • Estoy impaciente por verlos. I'm impatient to see them.
Use im- before roots starting with p or b (imposible, impaciente), and in- before everything else (inseguro, increíble). This mirrors English exactly.
When you see an unknown word, try stripping these patterns. Increíblemente = in- + creíble + -mente → not + believable + -ly → incredibly.
