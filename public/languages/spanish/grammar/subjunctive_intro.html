Subjunctive Intro — espero que, quiero que

The present subjunctive is a mood (not a tense) used to express wishes, emotions, doubt, and hypotheticals. The most practical entry point: when a main clause with an emotion or wish verb is followed by que, the next verb goes into the subjunctive.

The trigger pattern

Main clause [emotion/wish verb] + que + subordinate clause [subjunctive verb]

Trigger (main clause)Meaning
espero queI hope that
quiero queI want (you/him) to
me alegra queI'm glad that
tengo miedo de queI'm afraid that
ojalá (que)I hope / hopefully (from Arabic "may God wish")
es bueno queit's good that

Formation: the yo-form trick

Take the yo present tense → drop the -o → add opposite vowel endings:
-AR verbs take -e endings (hable, hables, hable, hablemos, hablen)
-ER / -IR verbs take -a endings (coma, comas, coma, comamos, coman)

Examples with emotion triggers

  • Espero que te sientas mejor. I hope you feel better.
  • Quiero que estés tranquilo. I want you to be calm.
  • Me alegra que estés aquí. I'm glad you're here.
  • Tengo miedo de que sea grave. I'm afraid it might be serious.
  • ¡Ojalá que todo salga bien! I hope everything goes well!
  • Es bueno que hables con él. It's good that you talk with him.

Key rule: different subjects required

The subjunctive is triggered when the subject changes between the two clauses. If the subject is the same, use an infinitive instead.

  • Quiero descansar. (same subject → infinitive) I want to rest.
  • Quiero que descanses. (different subject → subjunctive) I want you to rest.
