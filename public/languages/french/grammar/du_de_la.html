Partitive Articles — du, de la, de l', des

The partitive article expresses an unspecified, partial quantity of something — the idea of "some" in English, though English often omits it. In French, the partitive is obligatory when talking about an unspecified amount of something, especially food, drink, and uncountable nouns.

Forms of the Partitive Article

ContextPartitiveExample
Masculine singulardu (= de + le)du pain, du café, du riz
Feminine singularde lade la soupe, de la viande, de la crème
Before vowel or mute hde l'de l'eau, de l'huile, de l'argent
Plural (countable "some")desdes fruits, des légumes, des amis

When to Use the Partitive

Use the partitive when you mean some — an unspecified portion of something you could theoretically measure or scoop out:

  • Je veux du pain. I want (some) bread.
  • Elle boit de la limonade. She's drinking (some) lemonade.
  • On mange de l'agneau ce soir. We're eating (some) lamb tonight.
  • Il y a de la musique dans le café. There is (some) music in the café.
  • J'ai de l'argent. I have (some) money.

Partitive vs Definite vs Indefinite

ArticleMeaningExample
du / de la / de l' (partitive)some (unspecified amount)Je mange du fromage. (I'm eating some cheese.)
le / la / l' / les (definite)the (specific) or in generalJ'aime le fromage. (I like cheese in general.)
un / une (indefinite)a / an (one unit)Je veux un fromage. (I want one/a cheese.)

After Negation: de / d'

After a negation (ne...pas), all partitive and indefinite articles change to simply de (or d' before a vowel):

  • Je mange du pain. I eat bread. → Je ne mange pas de pain. I don't eat bread.
  • Il a de la chance. He has luck. → Il n'a pas de chance. He has no luck.
  • Elle boit de l'eau. She drinks water. → Elle ne boit pas d'eau. She doesn't drink water.

Exception: The definite article does NOT change after negation: "Je n'aime pas le café." (I don't like coffee.)

After Expressions of Quantity

After quantity expressions, use only de (never du/de la/des):

  • beaucoup de café a lot of coffee
  • un peu de lait a little milk
  • trop de sel too much salt
  • assez de pain enough bread
  • un verre d'eau a glass of water

Common Pitfalls

  • English often omits "some" — but French cannot omit the partitive. "I want bread" = Je veux du pain (never just Je veux pain).
  • Don't confuse du (partitive de + le) with du (preposition de + le, meaning "of the"): le prix du café = the price of the coffee (not partitive).
  • Abstract nouns can also take the partitive: avoir du courage (to have courage), faire de la musique (to make music).
