Qui et Que — Relative Pronouns

Relative pronouns link a main clause to a relative clause that gives more information about a noun. qui and que both mean "who / that / which" — the choice depends on the grammatical role in the relative clause.

Qui — Subject Relative Pronoun

Use qui when the relative pronoun is the subject of the relative clause (i.e., it does the action). Qui is never elided.

  • le médecin qui soigne des patients the doctor who treats patients
  • l'ami qui m'a aidé the friend who helped me
  • le bureau qui est ouvert le samedi the office that is open on Saturdays

Que — Object Relative Pronoun

Use que when the relative pronoun is the direct object of the relative clause (i.e., something is done to it). Que elides to qu' before a vowel or mute h.

  • le travail que je fais the work that I do
  • la personne qu'il cherche the person he is looking for
  • le film qu'on a vu the film that we saw

How to Choose: Quick Test

QuestionResult
Does the relative clause already have its own subject + verb? → the noun being replaced is the objectUse que
Is the noun the one doing the action (no other subject in the clause)?Use qui

Que Cannot Be Omitted

In English, "that" can be dropped ("the book I read"). In French, que is always required: le livre que j'ai lu (never "le livre j'ai lu").

Past Participle Agreement with Que

In passé composé with avoir, the past participle agrees with a preceding direct object. When que precedes, the participle agrees with the antecedent of que.

  • la lettre qu'il a écrite the letter that he wrote (lettre is feminine → écrite)
  • les livres que tu as lus the books that you read (livres is masc. pl. → lus)
