Reflexive Verbs — se...

Reflexive verbs (verbes pronominaux) describe actions that the subject does to itself. In French, these verbs are always paired with a reflexive pronoun (me, te, se, nous, vous, se) that reflects the action back to the subject. They are extremely common in French — especially for daily routines, emotions, and movement.

Negation

In negation, ne comes before the reflexive pronoun and pas comes after the verb:

  • Je ne me lève pas tôt. I don't get up early.
  • Elle ne s'inquiète pas. She doesn't worry.

Body Parts with Reflexive Verbs

When reflexive verbs are used with body parts, French uses the definite article (le/la/les), not a possessive, because the reflexive pronoun already shows ownership:

  • Je me lave les mains. I wash my hands. (not "mes mains")
  • Il se brosse les dents. He brushes his teeth.
  • Je me suis cassé le bras. I broke my arm.

Passé Composé — Reflexive Verbs use Être

All reflexive verbs form the passé composé with être (not avoir), and the past participle agrees with the subject in most cases:

  • Je me suis levé(e). I got up.
  • Elle s'est couchée tard. She went to bed late.
  • Ils se sont réveillés tôt. They woke up early.

Common Pitfalls

  • The reflexive pronoun must match the subject — never use the wrong one. Je se lave is wrong; Je me lave is correct.
  • In the infinitive, the reflexive pronoun changes to match the intended subject when used after another verb: Je veux me lever. (I want to get up.) — not "Je veux se lever."
  • Many verbs that are reflexive in French are not reflexive in English: s'appeler (to be called), se souvenir (to remember), se tromper (to be mistaken).
