Avoir — To Have

Avoir (to have) is the second of the two most fundamental French verbs. Beyond literal possession, avoir is used for many expressions of physical and emotional states that English expresses with "to be." It is also the most common auxiliary verb for forming compound past tenses.

Key Uses of Avoir

1. Possession

  • J'ai un livre. I have a book.
  • Elle a une voiture. She has a car.
  • Nous avons deux chats. We have two cats.

2. Physical states (where English uses "to be")

  • J'ai faim. I'm hungry. (lit. I have hunger)
  • Tu as soif. You're thirsty. (lit. you have thirst)
  • Il a froid. He's cold. (lit. he has cold)
  • Elle a chaud. She's hot. (lit. she has warmth)
  • J'ai peur. I'm scared. (lit. I have fear)
  • Il a honte. He's ashamed. (lit. he has shame)

3. Age

  • J'ai vingt-cinq ans. I'm twenty-five years old. (lit. I have twenty-five years)
  • Quel âge as-tu ? How old are you? (lit. What age do you have?)

4. Avoir besoin de — To need

  • J'ai besoin d'aide. I need help.
  • On a besoin de temps. We need time.

5. Auxiliary for passé composé (most verbs)

  • J'ai mangé. I ate / I have eaten.
  • Tu as vu le film ? Did you see the film?
  • Nous avons fini. We finished.

Common Pitfalls

  • Never "être faim": Physical states like hunger, thirst, and temperature use avoir, not être. Je suis faim is wrong; J'ai faim is correct.
  • Il y a (there is / there are) is built from avoir: literally "it has there." Il y a un café ici. (There is a café here.)
  • Past participle agreement: In passé composé with avoir, the past participle agrees with a preceding direct object: La pomme que j'ai mangée. (The apple that I ate.)
