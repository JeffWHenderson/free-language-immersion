Regular -ER Verbs

The largest group of French verbs ends in -ER. These verbs are called "regular" because they all follow the same conjugation pattern in the present tense. Once you learn this pattern, you can conjugate hundreds of verbs.

The Pattern — Drop -ER, Add the Endings

Take the infinitive, remove the -er, and add the following endings:

PronounEndingparler (to speak)manger (to eat)travailler (to work)
je-eje parleje mangeje travaille
tu-estu parlestu mangestu travailles
il/elle/on-eil parleelle mangeon travaille
nous-onsnous parlonsnous mangeonsnous travaillons
vous-ezvous parlezvous mangezvous travaillez
ils/elles-entils parlentelles mangentils travaillent

Note: The endings -e, -es, -ent are all silent! "je parle", "tu parles", "ils parlent" all sound the same: /parl/.

Examples in Sentences

  • Je parle français. I speak French.
  • Tu travailles ici ? Do you work here?
  • Elle aime le café. She likes coffee.
  • On habite à Lyon. We live in Lyon.
  • Vous parlez anglais ? Do you speak English?
  • Ils mangent ensemble. They eat together.

Spelling Changes in Some -ER Verbs

A few -ER verbs have minor spelling changes to preserve pronunciation:

Verb typeRuleExample
Verbs ending in -ger (manger, voyager)Add e before -ons: nous mangeonsnous mangeons, nous voyageons
Verbs ending in -cer (commencer, placer)Change c → ç before -ons: nous commençonsnous commençons, nous plaçons
Verbs with é in stem (préférer, espérer)é → è before silent endings: je préfèreje préfère, tu préfères

Common Pitfalls

  • Silent endings: The -s in "tu parles" and the -ent in "ils parlent" are never pronounced. Beginners often mispronounce these.
  • Nous vs On: In spoken French, on is used instead of nous most of the time and takes the same ending as il/elle: on mange (we eat).
  • Not all verbs ending in -er are regular: aller (to go) is completely irregular.
