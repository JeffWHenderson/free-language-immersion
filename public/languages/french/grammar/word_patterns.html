Word Patterns — Spotting How French Works

French is full of repeating patterns that unlock hundreds of words at once. Once you recognise these five patterns, you will be able to guess the meaning and form of new words with confidence — and you will spot the enormous overlap with English.

1. -ment — Turning Adjectives into Adverbs

The suffix -ment works like English -ly: it turns an adjective into an adverb. The rule is to take the feminine form of the adjective and add -ment.

Adjective (masc.)Feminine formAdverbMeaning

sérieuxsérieusesérieusementseriously

parfaitparfaiteparfaitementperfectly
  • Il parle lentement. He speaks slowly.
  • Heureusement, il est arrivé. Fortunately, he arrived.
  • C'est parfaitement normal. It's perfectly normal.
Exception — -amment / -emment: Adjectives ending in -ant or -ent use special endings instead of the feminine rule:
  • courant → couramment (fluently)
  • évident → évidemment (obviously / evidently)
Both endings are pronounced the same: -amment.

2. Past Participle Patterns (-é, -i, -u + irregulars)

The past participle is formed predictably for regular verbs, and must be memorised for the most common irregular ones. It is used in the passé composé and also as an adjective.

Verb groupRuleExampleParticiple
-ER verbsreplace -er with -éparlerparlé
-IR verbsreplace -ir with -ifinirfini
-RE verbsreplace -re with -uvendrevendu

Key irregular past participles to memorise:

  • faire → fait done / made
  • être → été been
  • avoir → eu had
  • prendre → pris taken
  • dire → dit said
  • voir → vu seen
  • venir → venu come
  • pouvoir → pu been able to
  • savoir → su known
Past participles also work as adjectives. When used this way, they agree in gender and number with the noun:
  • une porte fermée a closed door
  • un travail bien fait a job well done

3. -tion / -sion — Abstract Nouns

French -tion and -sion nouns are almost identical to their English counterparts. These are all feminine nouns.

  • La situation est compliquée. The situation is complicated.
  • J'aime la conversation. I enjoy conversation.
Vocabulary shortcut: Thousands of English -tion words exist in French with minimal spelling changes. If you know the English word, you almost certainly know the French word — just remember it is feminine and pronounce the ending as -syon.

4. -eur / -euse — Person Nouns (Professions & Doers)

Adding -eur (masculine) or -euse (feminine) to a verb stem creates a noun meaning "the person who does that action". Some professions use -teur / -trice instead.

-teur / -trice
  • directeur / directrice director / directress
  • acteur / actrice actor / actress
-eur / -euse
  • chanteur / chanteuse male / female singer
  • vendeur / vendeuse male / female salesperson
-eur is the masculine form. The feminine is almost always -euse, or -trice for words ending in -teur. Notice how many of these are instantly recognisable from English: acteur, directeur, professeur.

5. Prefix Patterns: re-, dé-/dis-, in-/im-/ir-

French prefixes work just like English ones and let you build new words from ones you already know.

re- (again / back)

  • lire → relire to read → to reread
  • faire → refaire to do → to redo
  • commencer → recommencer to start → to restart

dé- / dis- (undo / opposite)

  • habiller → déshabiller to dress → to undress
  • connecter → déconnecter to connect → to disconnect
  • paraître → disparaître to appear → to disappear

in- / im- / ir- (not)

  • possible → impossible possible → impossible
  • connu → inconnu known → unknown
  • responsable → irresponsable responsible → irresponsible
French and English share thousands of cognates. -tion words, -ment adverbs, and -eur person nouns are almost always recognisable from English.
