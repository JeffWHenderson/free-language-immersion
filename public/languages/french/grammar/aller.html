Aller — To Go

Aller (to go) is one of the most irregular and most frequently used verbs in French. Beyond movement, aller + infinitive forms the futur proche (near future), the most common way to talk about plans and intentions.

1. Going to a Place

Aller is followed by à (or its contracted forms) when indicating destination:

  • Je vais au café. I'm going to the café. (à + le = au)
  • Elle va à la bibliothèque. She's going to the library.
  • Nous allons à l'hôpital. We're going to the hospital.
  • Ils vont aux États-Unis. They're going to the United States. (à + les = aux)

2. The Near Future — Aller + Infinitive

Aller + infinitive expresses what is going to happen — plans, intentions, or immediate future actions. This is more common in spoken French than the simple future tense.

  • Je vais manger. I'm going to eat.
  • Tu vas comprendre. You're going to understand.
  • On va partir à midi. We're going to leave at noon.
  • Ils vont arriver demain. They're going to arrive tomorrow.
  • Elle ne va pas venir. She's not going to come.

3. Greeting — Comment ça va?

Aller is used idiomatically to ask about well-being:

  • Comment allez-vous ? How are you? (formal)
  • Comment tu vas ? / Ça va ? How are you? (informal)
  • Ça va bien, merci. I'm doing well, thanks.
  • Ça ne va pas. I'm not doing well. / That doesn't work.

Common Pitfalls

  • Aller vs Partir: aller = to go (toward a destination); partir = to leave (from a place). Je vais à l'école (I'm going to school) vs Je pars de la maison (I'm leaving the house).
  • Don't skip the infinitive: The near future always needs aller + infinitive. You cannot say just Je vais manger and then use a noun — the infinitive must follow directly.
  • Aller in negation: In the near future, ne...pas wraps around aller, not the infinitive: Je ne vais pas partir. (I'm not going to leave.)
