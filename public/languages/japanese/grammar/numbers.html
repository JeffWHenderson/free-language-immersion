Numbers & Counters

Japanese has two number systems that work side by side: Sino-Japanese numbers (borrowed from Chinese) used with most counters, time, money, and large quantities, and native Japanese numbers used for standalone counting of objects 1–10.

Building Larger Numbers

Numbers are built by combining units — no word for "and" is needed. Hundreds and thousands that start with one (1) drop the 一 (いち) before 百 and 千 in natural speech.

Native Japanese Numbers (1–10)

Use native Japanese numbers when counting standalone items without a specific counter, or with counters like 〜つ. They only go up to ten — above ten, always use Sino-Japanese.

  • ひとつ one (thing)
  • ふたつ two (things)
  • みっつ three (things)
  • よっつ four (things)
  • いつつ five (things)
  • むっつ six (things)
  • ななつ seven (things)
  • やっつ eight (things)
  • ここのつ nine (things)
  • とお ten (things)

Which System to Use

Sino-Japanese

Time (〜時), money (〜円), floors (〜階), minutes (〜分), phone numbers, large quantities

Native Japanese

Standalone counting of unspecified objects (1–10), items without a specialized counter

Common Counters

CounterUsed forExample
〜個 (こ)General small objectsりんご三個 (3 apples)
〜本 (ほん)Long, thin things (bottles, pens, rivers)水を二本 (2 bottles of water)
〜枚 (まい)Flat things (paper, tickets, plates)切符二枚 (2 tickets)
〜匹 (ひき)Small animals猫三匹 (3 cats)
〜冊 (さつ)Books, notebooks本一冊 (1 book)
〜杯 (はい)Cups, glasses, drinksコーヒー一杯 (1 cup of coffee)
Sound changes: Counters often trigger sound changes. 一本 → いっぽん, 六本 → ろっぽん, 八本 → はっぽん. 一個 → いっこ, 一冊 → いっさつ. These patterns are best learned through practice.

Examples

  • 水を二本ください。 Two bottles of water, please. (Mizu wo ni-hon kudasai.)
  • 何時ですか？三時です。 What time is it? It's 3 o'clock. (Nanji desu ka? San-ji desu.)
  • 二千円です。 It's 2,000 yen. (Nisen-en desu.)
  • りんごをみっつください。 Three apples, please. (Ringo wo mittsu kudasai.)
