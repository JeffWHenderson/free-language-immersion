〜たら / 〜ば — Conditionals (If / When)

Japanese has two main conditional forms: 〜たら and 〜ば. Both express "if" or "when", but they differ in nuance and formality. 〜たら is the most common in everyday speech; 〜ば carries a more hypothetical, literary feel. Adding もし at the start of the sentence adds extra emphasis to the condition.

At a glance

FormHow to make itNuance

〜ばば-form: u-verb → e + ば (行く → 行けば); ru-verb → れば (食べる → 食べれば)Hypothetical, formal or literary; often used for conditions that may not be met

〜たら — natural conditional

Form: take the plain past form and add ら. Use 〜たら when something is likely to happen and a result follows. It is the default conditional for everyday conversation.

  • もし疲れたら、休んでください。 Moshi tsukaretara, yasunde kudasai. — If you're tired, please rest.
  • 雨が降ったら、家にいます。 Ame ga futtara, ie ni imasu. — If it rains, I'll stay home.
  • 宿題が終わったら、遊んでいいよ。 Shukudai ga owattara, asonde ii yo. — When you finish your homework, you can play.
  • 春になったら、花見に行こう。 Haru ni nattara, hanami ni ikō. — When spring comes, let's go flower viewing.

〜ば — hypothetical conditional

The ば-form is built differently depending on the verb group. Use 〜ば for hypothetical or counterfactual conditions — often when wishing something were different, or reasoning about ideals. It appears frequently in writing, proverbs, and formal speech.

Verb typeRuleExample

Group 2 (ru-verbs)る → れば食べる → 食べれば / 起きる → 起きれば
Irregularする → すれば / くる → くれば勉強すれば / 来れば

  • もっとお金があれば、旅行できる。 Motto okane ga areba, ryokō dekiru. — If I had more money, I could travel.
  • 早く起きれば、電車に間に合う。 Hayaku okireba, densha ni ma ni au. — If you get up early, you'll make the train.
  • 練習すれば、上手になれる。 Renshū sureba, jōzu ni nareru. — If you practice, you can get better.

〜たら vs. 〜ば — what is the difference?

〜たら〜ば
RegisterConversational, widely usedFormal, literary, or hypothetical
FocusSequential: X happens, then YLogical condition: only if X is met, Y holds
With wishesOK (e.g. 〜たらよかった = I wish I had…)More natural for hypotheticals not yet real
Resultative YY can be a command or requestY should not be a direct command (unnatural)

もし — adding emphasis

もし (moshi) means "if (by any chance)" and can precede either 〜たら or 〜ば to stress that the condition is uncertain or hypothetical. It is optional, but common when the speaker wants to soften or highlight the "if".

  • もし気分が悪くなったら、すぐ言ってね。 Moshi kibun ga waruku nattara, sugu itte ne. — If you start feeling unwell, tell me right away.
  • もし時間があれば、一緒に来てください。 Moshi jikan ga areba, issho ni kite kudasai. — If you happen to have time, please come with us.

Emotion context examples

  • 悲しかったら、泣いてもいいよ。 Kanashikattara, naite mo ii yo. — If you're sad, it's okay to cry.
  • もし嬉しければ、笑顔を見せて。 Moshi ureshikereba, egao wo misete. — If you're happy, show me your smile.
  • 心配だったら、話してください。 Shinpai dattara, hanashite kudasai. — If you're worried, please talk to me.

Usage notes

  • 〜たら is by far more common in spoken Japanese. When in doubt, use 〜たら.
  • 〜ば is preferred in written Japanese, proverbs, and when expressing hypothetical or counterfactual situations.
  • You cannot use 〜ば when the result clause is a request or command directed at the listener — use 〜たら instead: ○ 疲れたら休んで ✗ 疲れれば休んで.
  • 〜たらよかった expresses regret: もっと勉強したらよかった (I should have studied more).
  • もし is an adverb, not a conjunction — it modifies the whole conditional clause, not the result.
