〜られる — Passive Voice

The passive voice in Japanese expresses that the subject receives or experiences an action done by someone else. The agent (the one doing the action) is marked by に. Japanese also has an "adversative passive" — used when something unpleasant happens to you — which has no direct equivalent in English. Mastering the passive is essential for describing work situations, feedback, and events that affect you.

Basic passive — agent marked with に

The basic passive describes an action received by the subject. The person performing the action is marked by に.

  • 先生に褒められました。 Sensei ni homeraremashita. — I was praised by the teacher.
  • マネージャーに仕事を頼まれました。 Manēja ni shigoto wo tanomaremashita. — I was asked to do work by the manager.
  • このレポートは部長に書かれました。 Kono repōto wa buchō ni kakaremashita. — This report was written by the department head.
  • その映画はたくさんの人に見られています。 Sono eiga wa takusan no hito ni mirarete imasu. — That film is being watched by many people.

Adversative passive — when something unpleasant happens to you

The adversative passive describes an event that negatively affected the speaker, even if unintentionally. English often requires a full re-phrasing ("My car was stolen" or "I had my wallet stolen"), but Japanese uses the same passive form with a nuance of inconvenience or suffering.

  • 財布を盗まれました。 Saifu wo nusumaremashita. — My wallet was stolen (and I suffered from it).
  • 雨に降られて、びしょ濡れになった。 Ame ni furarete, bisho nure ni natta. — It rained on me and I got soaked.
  • 同僚に仕事を押し付けられた。 Dōryō ni shigoto wo oshitsuke rareta. — My coworker dumped their work on me.
  • 会議中に電話をかけられて困った。 Kaigichū ni denwa wo kakerarete komatta. — Someone called me during the meeting and it was inconvenient.

Work context examples

  • 新しいプロジェクトを任されました。 Atarashii purojekuto wo makasaremashita. — I was entrusted with a new project.
  • 上司に叱られてしまいました。 Jōshi ni shikarare te shimaimashita. — I ended up being scolded by my boss.
  • チームに助けられて、プロジェクトを終えることができました。 Chīmu ni tasukerarete, purojekuto wo oeru koto ga dekimashita. — With help from the team, I was able to finish the project.
  • お客様に感謝されて、とても嬉しかったです。 Okyakusama ni kansha sarete, totemo ureshikatta desu. — I was thanked by the customer and felt very happy.

Potential vs. passive — Group 2 verbs

For Group 2 (ru-verbs), the passive form られる is identical to the potential form られる. Context usually makes the meaning clear, but be aware of the overlap.

Form食べられる見られる
Passive meaningis eaten (by someone)is seen / is watched (by someone)
Potential meaningcan eatcan see / can watch
How to tell apartPassive usually has a に-marked agent; potential focuses on ability
  • 魚が食べられます。（潜在能力） Sakana ga taberaremasu. (potential) — I can eat fish.
  • 魚が猫に食べられました。（受身） Sakana ga neko ni taberaremashita. (passive) — The fish was eaten by the cat.

Usage notes

  • The passive form conjugates like a Group 2 (ru-verb): 書かれる → 書かれます / 書かれた / 書かれない.
  • に marks the agent in passive sentences. By (with) a tool or means uses で instead: ハサミで切られた (cut with scissors).
  • The adversative passive is common in daily conversation when describing misfortune, inconvenience, or unwanted events that affected the speaker.
  • In formal or written Japanese, passive voice is used extensively — much more than in English — to sound objective or impersonal.
