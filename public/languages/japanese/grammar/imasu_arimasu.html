います vs あります — To Exist

Japanese has two verbs meaning "to exist" or "to have": います (imasu) and あります (arimasu). The choice depends entirely on whether the thing being referred to is animate (alive and capable of self-movement) or inanimate (stationary, not self-moving). This distinction is not about counting or mass — it is about life.

The Core Rule

VerbUsed forExamples
います (imasu)People, animals — animate beingspeople, cats, fish, birds, insects
あります (arimasu)Objects, plants, events — inanimate thingsbooks, cars, trees, meetings, money

Basic Pattern

[Location] に [Subject] が います / あります。

  • こうえんにこどもがいます。 There are children in the park. (Kōen ni kodomo ga imasu.)
  • テーブルのうえにほんがあります。 There is a book on the table. (Tēburu no ue ni hon ga arimasu.)
  • いえにねこがいます。 There is a cat at home. (Ie ni neko ga imasu.)
  • れいぞうこにたまごがあります。 There are eggs in the refrigerator. (Reizōko ni tamago ga arimasu.)

います — Animate Beings

  • かれはどこにいますか？ Where is he? (Kare wa doko ni imasu ka?)
  • むすめがふたりいます。 I have two daughters. (Musume ga futari imasu.)
  • にわにとりがいます。 There is a bird in the garden. (Niwa ni tori ga imasu.)
  • うみにさかながたくさんいます。 There are many fish in the sea. (Umi ni sakana ga takusan imasu.)

あります — Inanimate Things

  • ちかくにスーパーがありますか？ Is there a supermarket nearby? (Chikaku ni sūpā ga arimasu ka?)
  • じかんがあります。 I have time. (Jikan ga arimasu.)
  • もんだいがあります。 There is a problem. (Mondai ga arimasu.)
  • あしたかいぎがあります。 There is a meeting tomorrow. (Ashita kaigi ga arimasu.)

Negatives

PositiveNegativeMeaning
いますいませんis not / are not (animate)
ありますありませんis not / are not (inanimate)
  • だれもいません。 There is no one. (Dare mo imasen.)
  • おかねがありません。 I have no money. (Okane ga arimasen.)

Common Mistakes

  • Plants use あります. Trees and flowers cannot move by themselves — use あります: にわにきがあります (there is a tree in the garden).
  • Robots and toys use あります (usually). Even if they move, they are inanimate objects. Context and speaker intent matter, but the default is あります.
  • "To have" uses the same verbs. いぬがいます = "I have a dog" or "there is a dog." Japanese does not have a separate verb for possession of living things vs. location.
