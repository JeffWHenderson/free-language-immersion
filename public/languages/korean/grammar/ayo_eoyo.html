아요/어요 — Polite Verb Ending

The 아요/어요 (ayo/eoyo) ending is the most essential verb ending in Korean. It creates the informal polite present tense — the register you'll use most often in daily life: with strangers, shopkeepers, acquaintances, and in most public situations.

To form this ending, you drop 다 (da) from the dictionary form and add either 아요 or 어요 depending on the final vowel of the verb stem.

Important: 하다 verbs

Verbs ending in 하다 (hada) — which are very common in Korean — have a special shortcut: 하다 always becomes 해요 (haeyo).

  • 공부하다 → 공부해요 (gongbuhada → gongbuhaeyo) — to study → I study
  • 요리하다 → 요리해요 (yorihada → yorihaeyo) — to cook → I cook
  • 일하다 → 일해요 (ilhada → ilhaeyo) — to work → I work

This one ending handles multiple functions

아요/어요 expresses present, ongoing, or habitual action. It also functions as a question when your intonation rises.

  • 저는 밥을 먹어요. (Jeoneun babeul meogeoyo.) — I eat rice / I am eating rice.
  • 밥을 먹어요? (Babeul meogeoyo?) — Are you eating rice? / Do you eat rice?
  • 저는 서울에 살아요. (Jeoneun seoule sarayo.) — I live in Seoul.
  • 한국어를 공부해요. (Hangugeoreul gongbuhaeyo.) — I study Korean.
  • 커피를 마셔요. (Keopireul masyeoyo.) — I drink coffee.
  • 음악을 들어요. (Eumagul deoreoyo.) — I listen to music.

Common mistakes

  • Don't use 아요 when 어요 is needed. Check the last vowel of the stem — if it's not 아 or 오, use 어요.
  • 하다 verbs always become 해요, never 하아요 or 하어요.
  • When stems end in a vowel, the vowel and 아요/어요 often contract or merge — this is expected and natural.
  • This ending does not change based on person or number — 저는 먹어요 / 그녀는 먹어요 / 우리는 먹어요 all use the same form.
