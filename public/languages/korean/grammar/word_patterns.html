Word Patterns — Spotting How Korean Works

Korean has powerful, consistent patterns that let you build vocabulary rapidly. Once you recognise these five patterns you will start seeing them everywhere — in news headlines, menus, signs, and everyday speech. Learning a pattern is worth far more than memorising isolated words.

1. -하다 — Turning Nouns into Verbs

Thousands of Sino-Korean nouns become verbs simply by adding 하다 (hada). This is one of the most productive patterns in the language — whenever you learn a noun, ask yourself whether it can take 하다.

NounMeaningVerbMeaning
공부 (gongbu)study공부하다 (gongbuhada)to study
사랑 (sarang)love사랑하다 (saranghada)to love
운동 (undong)exercise운동하다 (undonghada)to exercise
요리 (yori)cooking요리하다 (yorihada)to cook
행복 (haengbok)happiness행복하다 (haengbokhada)to be happy
청소 (cheongso)cleaning청소하다 (cheongsohada)to clean

In polite speech, 하다 becomes 해요 (haeyo): 공부해요 (I study), 사랑해요 (I love you), 청소해요 (I clean).

  • 저는 매일 운동해요. (Jeoneun maeil undonghaeyo.) — I exercise every day.
  • 요리하는 것을 좋아해요. (Yorihaneun geoseul joahaeyo.) — I like cooking.
  • 지금 청소해요. (Jigeum cheongsohaeyo.) — I'm cleaning right now.

2. -적(인) — Adjective Suffix (like English -ic / -al)

The suffix 적 (jeok) turns nouns into adjectives, much like English -ic or -al. Add 인 (in) when the adjective comes directly before a noun.

NounMeaningAdjective (-적인)Meaning
사회 (sahoe)society사회적인 (sahoejeokin)social
문화 (munhwa)culture문화적인 (munhwajeokin)cultural
경제 (gyeongje)economy경제적인 (gyeongjejeokin)economic
역사 (yeoksa)history역사적인 (yeoksajeokin)historical
개인 (gaein)individual개인적인 (gaeinjeokin)personal / individual

적 alone is used before the particle 으로 (euro) to form adverbs: 사회적으로 (sahoejeogeuro) = socially, 경제적으로 (gyeongjejeogeuro) = economically.

  • 사회적인 문제예요. (Sahoejeokin munjeyeyo.) — It's a social problem.
  • 개인적인 질문이에요. (Gaeinjeokin jilmunieyo.) — It's a personal question.
  • 역사적으로 중요해요. (Yeoksajeogeuro jungyohaeyo.) — It's historically important.

3. 불- / 비- / 무- — Negative Prefixes

Korean uses three main prefixes to negate or reverse the meaning of a word. Learning these triples your vocabulary immediately.

불- (bul-) — impossible / bad / un-
  • 가능 → 불가능 (ganeung → bulganeung) — possible → impossible
  • 행복 → 불행 (haengbok → bulhaeng) — happiness → unhappiness
  • 편 → 불편 (pyeon → bulpyeon) — comfortable → inconvenient
비- (bi-) — non- / un- / unofficial
  • 공식 → 비공식 (gongsik → bigongsik) — official → unofficial
  • 공개 → 비공개 (gonggae → bigonggae) — public → private / undisclosed
무- (mu-) — without / -less
  • 관심 → 무관심 (gwansim → mugwansim) — interest → indifference
  • 조건 → 무조건 (jogeon → mujogeon) — condition → unconditional
  • 한 → 무한 (han → muhan) — limit → infinite / limitless

These prefixes come from Chinese (Sino-Korean) and attach to Sino-Korean roots. 불- is the most common in everyday speech; 비- is frequent in formal or news contexts; 무- often emphasises total absence.

4. -기 — Turning Verbs into Nouns

Adding 기 (gi) to a verb stem creates a noun meaning "the act of ___ing". This is how Korean expresses gerunds — the equivalent of English "-ing" used as a subject or object.

VerbMeaningNoun (-기)Meaning
먹다 (meokda)to eat먹기 (meokgi)eating
읽다 (ilkda)to read읽기 (ilkgi)reading
말하다 (malhada)to speak말하기 (malhagi)speaking
쓰다 (sseuda)to write쓰기 (sseugi)writing
  • 수영하기 좋아해요. (Suyeonghagi joahaeyo.) — I like swimming.
  • 한국어 말하기가 어려워요. (Hangueo malhagiga eoryeowoyo.) — Speaking Korean is hard.
  • 책 읽기를 좋아해요. (Chaek ilkkireul joahaeyo.) — I like reading books.

-기 is also used in fixed expressions: 쉽기 (easy to do), 어렵기 (hard to do). Compare with -(으)ㄴ/는 것 which is a more formal nominaliser — 기 feels more natural in everyday speech.

5. -(으)ㄹ 수 있다/없다 — Can / Can't

This pattern expresses ability or possibility. Attach (으)ㄹ 수 있다 to a verb stem for "can", and (으)ㄹ 수 없다 for "can't". In polite speech, 있다 becomes 있어요 and 없다 becomes 없어요.

Verb stem ends inForm to useExample
Vowel (e.g. 가, 하)-ㄹ 수 있다갈 수 있어요 (gal su isseoyo) — can go
Consonant (e.g. 먹, 읽)-을 수 있다먹을 수 있어요 (meogeul su isseoyo) — can eat
  • 갈 수 있어요. (Gal su isseoyo.) — I can go.
  • 먹을 수 없어요. (Meogeul su eopseoyo.) — I can't eat (it).
  • 할 수 있어요! (Hal su isseoyo!) — I can do it! / You can do it!
  • 한국어 말할 수 있어요. (Hangueo malhal su isseoyo.) — I can speak Korean.

못 (mot) before a verb is a shorter way to say "can't" in speech: 못 가요 = can't go. But -(으)ㄹ 수 없어요 is more precise and formal.
