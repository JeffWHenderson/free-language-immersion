이/가 아파요 — Expressing Pain

In Korean, pain and physical discomfort are expressed using the pattern: [body part] + 이/가 아파요. The adjective verb 아프다 (apeuda) means "to be painful / to hurt / to be sick." The body part that hurts takes the subject marker 이/가.

FormUsage
아파요 (apayo)Informal polite — everyday use
아픕니다 (apemnida)Formal polite — medical/official context
아파 (apa)Casual — with close friends

Pattern: [body part] + 이/가 + 아파요

The body part takes 이 after a consonant and 가 after a vowel.

Examples

  • 머리가 아파요. (Meoriga apayo.) — My head hurts. / I have a headache.
  • 배가 아파요. (Baega apayo.) — My stomach hurts.
  • 목이 아파요. (Mogi apayo.) — My throat hurts. / I have a sore throat.
  • 다리가 아파요. (Dariga apayo.) — My leg hurts.
  • 어디가 아파요? (Eodiga apayo?) — Where does it hurt? / What hurts?
  • 눈이 아파요. (Nuni apayo.) — My eyes hurt.
  • 온몸이 아파요. (Onmomi apayo.) — My whole body aches.

At the doctor

  • 어디가 아프세요? (Eodiga apeuseyo?) — Where are you in pain? [doctor asking, honorific]
  • 이틀 전부터 머리가 아팠어요. (Iteul jeonbuteo meoriga apasseoyo.) — My head has been hurting for two days.
  • 열이 있어요. (Yeori isseoyo.) — I have a fever.

Common mistakes

  • Don't use 을/를 (object marker) with the body part — it takes 이/가 (subject marker), because 아프다 is an adjective verb and the body part is its subject.
  • Don't say 머리를 아파요 — that's wrong. The correct form is 머리가 아파요.
  • 아프다 can also mean "to be sick" in general: 아파요 = "I'm sick / I don't feel well."
