있어요 vs 없어요 — Have / Don't Have

있어요 (isseoyo) and 없어요 (eopseoyo) are two of the most frequently used words in Korean. They serve a double purpose: expressing existence ("there is / there isn't") and possession ("I have / I don't have").

WordDictionary formPolite formMeaning
있다 (itda)있다있어요 (isseoyo)to exist / to have
없다 (eopda)없다없어요 (eopseoyo)not to exist / not to have

Expressing possession: "I have X"

Structure: [noun]이/가 있어요 — literally "X exists (for me)"

  • 돈이 있어요. (Doni isseoyo.) — I have money. (lit. Money exists.)
  • 시간이 없어요. (Sigani eopseoyo.) — I don't have time.
  • 질문이 있어요? (Jilmuni isseoyo?) — Do you have questions?
  • 한국 친구가 있어요. (Hanguk chinguga isseoyo.) — I have Korean friends.

Expressing existence: "There is X"

Structure: [place]에 [noun]이/가 있어요 — "X exists at [place]"

  • 카페에 사람이 많이 있어요. (Kapeе sarami mani isseoyo.) — There are many people in the café.
  • 냉장고에 우유가 없어요. (Naengjanggoe uyuga eopseoyo.) — There is no milk in the refrigerator.
  • 여기에 화장실이 있어요? (Yeogie hwajangsiri isseoyo?) — Is there a bathroom here?

있어요 as a progressive marker

있어요 also combines with the verb stem + 고 (go) to form the progressive tense ("is doing").

  • 먹고 있어요. (Meokgo isseoyo.) — I am eating.
  • 공부하고 있어요. (Gongbuhago isseoyo.) — I am studying.

Formal and casual forms

Register있다없다

Common mistakes

  • Don't say 가지고 있어요 (gajigo isseoyo, "holding/having") when simple 있어요 will do — 있어요 already expresses possession naturally.
  • Remember that the object of possession takes the subject marker 이/가, not the object marker 을/를. "I have water" → 물이 있어요, not 물을 있어요.
  • 있어요 and 없어요 are true opposites — there is no middle ground like "sort of have" using these verbs.
