을/를 — Object Marker

In Korean, the direct object of a verb is marked with 을 (eul) or 를 (reul). These particles attach directly to the noun that receives the action of the verb. English uses word order to show the object; Korean uses this particle.

MarkerUse afterExample nounWith marker
을 (eul)consonant밥 (bap, rice)밥을
를 (reul)vowel커피 (keopi, coffee)커피를

Sentence structure with 을/를

Korean word order is: Subject + Object + Verb. The object marker tells you what the verb is acting on, regardless of position.

Examples

  • 저는 커피를 마셔요. (Jeoneun keopireul masyeoyo.) — I drink coffee.
  • 그녀는 밥을 먹어요. (Geunyeoneun babeul meogeoyo.) — She eats rice.
  • 저는 한국어를 공부해요. (Jeoneun hangugeoreul gongbuhaeyo.) — I study Korean.
  • 음악을 들어요. (Eumagul deoreoyo.) — I listen to music.
  • 사과를 좋아해요. (Sagwareul joahaeyo.) — I like apples.
  • 책을 읽어요. (Chaegeul ilgeoyo.) — I read a book.
  • 친구를 만났어요. (Chingureul mannasseoyo.) — I met a friend.

When can you drop 을/를?

In casual spoken Korean, object markers are frequently dropped when context makes the meaning clear. The written language and more formal speech tend to keep them. As a learner, it's best to include them consistently until the pattern is automatic.

  • 커피 마셔요? (casual) → 커피를 마셔요? (standard) Do you drink coffee?

Common mistakes

  • Don't confuse 을/를 (object) with 이/가 (subject). The subject does the action; the object receives it.
  • 을/를 is only for direct objects. Indirect objects (e.g., "to someone") use different particles like 에게 (ege) or 한테 (hante).
  • Remember: 을 after a consonant, 를 after a vowel. A common learner error is using 를 after a consonant.
