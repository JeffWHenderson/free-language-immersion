내 vs 제 — My (Informal vs Formal)

Korean has two words for "my": 내 (nae) and 제 (je). The difference comes down to the speech level you're using — specifically, which first-person pronoun you're working from.

Pronoun "I"Possessive "my"RegisterUse with
나 (na)내 (nae)Casual / informalClose friends, people younger than you
저 (jeo)제 (je)Polite / formalStrangers, elders, professional settings

How they are formed

나 + 의 (ui, possessive particle) → 내 (contracted in everyday speech)
저 + 의 (ui) → 제 (contracted form)

The full forms 나의 (naui) and 저의 (jeoui) exist but sound stiff and literary. In natural spoken Korean, 내 and 제 are used instead.

Examples with 내 (nae) — casual

  • 내 친구야. (Nae chinguya.) — (That's) my friend. [casual]
  • 내 핸드폰 어디 있어? (Nae haendeupohn eodi isseo?) — Where's my phone? [casual]
  • 내 방은 작아. (Nae bangeun jaga.) — My room is small. [casual]
  • 내 생일이야. (Nae saengiriya.) — It's my birthday. [casual]

Examples with 제 (je) — polite

  • 제 이름은 민준이에요. (Je ireumeun minjunieyo.) — My name is Minjun. [polite]
  • 제 친구를 소개할게요. (Je chingureul sogaehalkkeyo.) — I'll introduce my friend. [polite]
  • 이건 제 가방이에요. (Igeon je gabangieeyo.) — This is my bag. [polite]
  • 제 생각에는... (Je saenggageneun...) — In my opinion... [polite]

우리 (uri) — "our" used as "my"

There is an important cultural note: Koreans very commonly use 우리 (uri, "our") where English speakers would say "my" — especially for things shared with family or in a communal sense.

  • 우리 엄마 / 우리 아빠 (uri eomma / uri appa) — my mom / my dad (lit. our mom / our dad)
  • 우리 집 (uri jip) — my home (lit. our home)
  • 우리 학교 (uri hakgyo) — my school (lit. our school)

This usage reflects a Korean cultural sense of shared identity and belonging. Using 내 집 or 제 집 is not wrong, but 우리 집 sounds more natural in most contexts.

Common mistakes

  • Using 내 in formal or polite situations — always use 제 with strangers, at work, or when speaking to elders.
  • Using 나의 or 저의 in everyday speech — these are grammatically correct but sound unnatural. Use the contracted forms 내 and 제.
  • Forgetting about 우리 — over-using 제 집, 제 엄마 sounds slightly unnatural when the shared 우리 form is more culturally expected.
